  1. Sci. Data
    In-vivo non-contact multispectral oral disease image dataset with segmentation
    Sneha Chand, Karthik Namasivayam, Janak Dave, and 3 more authors
    Scientific Data, 2024
    Abs TL;DR HTML

    In imaging spectroscopy, gathering oral tissue spectral data from resected samples may not accurately represent tissue signatures due to time-dependent changes, blood loss, protein degeneration, and preservation chemicals. In-vivo spectral imaging is employed to address these limitations, but it poses challenges like device dimensions, tissue accessibility, and motion artifacts, impacting data quality and reliability. Our study publishes a dataset of spectral images focusing on oral diseases, addressing these challenges. We used a state-of-the-art multispectral camera, capturing images at 270*510 pixels resolution in 16 spectral bands (460 nm to 600 nm). The dataset includes 91 participants (15 healthy and 76 diseased), with multiple images per patient, totalling 243 spectral images. The dataset encompasses three oral health conditions: Oral Submucous Fibrosis (OSMF), Leukoplakia, and Oral Squamous Cell Carcinoma (OSCC). Detailed patient history records accompany each case. This publicly available oral health multispectral dataset has the potential to advance spectroscopy diagnosis. Integrating artificial intelligence with a comprehensive spectral signature repository holds promise for accurate disease analysis.

    Presents a publicly available in-vivo multispectral imaging dataset of oral diseases (OSMF, Leukoplakia, OSCC) captured across 16 spectral bands from 91 participants, enabling AI-based oral disease diagnosis research.

  2. MeMeA
    Design and Characterisation of Stereo Endoscope for Polyp Size Measurement
    GS Rahul, Janak Dave, SP Preejith, and 1 more author
    In 2024 IEEE International Symposium on Medical Measurements and Applications (MeMeA), 2024
    Abs TL;DR HTML

    Stereo imaging in endoscopy is an advancement in medical imaging that enhances depth perception while viewing anatomical structures. Depth perception helps improve surgical navigation and diagnostic accuracy and enables polyp size measurement. This paper addresses the design challenges of creating stereo endoscopes with dual cameras, balancing baseline width optimization for depth accuracy with adequate field-of-view overlap in confined spaces. A VGG16-based algorithm is proposed for disparity calculation, achieving synchronous image acquisition at 60 frames per second and demonstrating polyp measurement with an error rate of less than 5%.

    Designs a stereo endoscope system using dual cameras and a VGG16-based disparity algorithm for depth-based polyp size measurement, achieving less than 5% error rate at 60 fps.

  3. MeMeA
    Characterization of Blue Light Imaging for Early Cancer Detection: Validation and in Vivo Feasibility Study
    Shubham Sharma, Janak Dave, A Maheswari, and 2 more authors
    In 2024 IEEE International Symposium on Medical Measurements and Applications (MeMeA), 2024
    Abs TL;DR HTML

    The timely detection of gastrointestinal cancer (GI) is crucial for effective treatment, and endoscopy plays a crucial role in visualising the internal organs for abnormalities. Despite its utility, detecting polyps or early-stage cancer remains a challenge due to their subtle nature, often requiring subjective assessment by experienced clinicians. The introduction of Blue-Light Imaging (BLI) has emerged as a promising solution, accentuating structural tissues and abnormal growths. It uses the principle of light-tissue interaction to enhance the visualisation of the surface microvessels to localise the cancer. However, there are no quantitative studies that suggest a ratio of White:Blue light for visualising these structures with high contrast. In this paper, we comprehensively evaluate different ratios of White:Blue light intensity that can be used for enhanced endoscopic examinations. A gastroscope equipped with BLI technology was engineered, and experiments were carried out to assess the qualitative and quantitative performance of the images taken with different White:Blue light ratios. The system design, in vivo validation on the lower lip mucosa, and in vivo feasibility via pilot human study assessments are discussed. Analysis of our results suggests that mixing White:Blue in the ratio of 1:1 to 4:1 can provide a well-illuminated and high-contrast image. The findings from this study can help design and develop newer BLI systems to improve cancer diagnostics.

    Engineers a gastroscope with Blue-Light Imaging and evaluates White:Blue light intensity ratios, finding 1:1 to 4:1 ratios produce optimal high-contrast images for early GI cancer detection.

  4. SPIE
    Enhanced visualisation of major blood vessels using multispectral NIR imaging for surgical safety
    Janak Dave, Antony Raj, Mohanasankar Sivaprakasam, and 1 more author
    In Medical Imaging 2024: Clinical and Biomedical Imaging, 2024
    Abs TL;DR HTML

    Iatrogenic vascular injuries during surgery pose significant risks. This work applies multispectral near-infrared (NIR) imaging to enhance visualization of major blood vessels during surgical procedures. By leveraging the differential absorption of haemoglobin at multiple NIR wavelengths, the proposed method improves vessel-to-tissue contrast, enabling surgeons to better identify and avoid critical vascular structures. The system is validated through experiments demonstrating improved surgical safety through enhanced blood vessel visualization.

    Applies multispectral NIR imaging to enhance visualization of major blood vessels during surgery, improving vessel-to-tissue contrast to reduce iatrogenic vascular injury risk.

  1. EMBC
    Multispectral imaging for vein localization and contrast enhancement
    Janak Dave, Sneha Chand, Antony Raj, and 3 more authors
    In 2023 45th Annual International Conference of the IEEE Engineering in Medicine & Biology Society (EMBC), 2023
    Abs TL;DR HTML

    Intravenous (IV) catheterization is a common procedure. Still, there is a 26% chance of the first attempt catheterization failure due to the changing visibility of veins because of the patient’s skin tone and body fat content. Ultrasound assistive devices help locate deeper veins but are not practical in emergencies, and transillumination assistive devices have a low field of view. Commercial near-infrared (NIR) imaging devices are effective in vein localization but are expensive and are not used in low-cost clinical settings. To overcome this, NIR Multispectral Imaging (MSI) was used to find the optimal wavelength that provides the enhanced visualization of veins for all skin types and Body Mass Index (BMI). The band with the highest vein-to-skin contrast ratio was selected and contrast enhancement was done using our proposed method. The primary blocks of the proposed method are Gamma correction, Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE), Adaptive Thresholding, and image Fusion. The optimal spectral range was found to be 814-876 nm and our method increased the contrast by 0.41, 0.375, and 0.39 for fair, brown, and dark brown skin types, respectively, with different BMI.

    Uses multispectral NIR imaging to identify the optimal wavelength range (814-876 nm) for vein visualization across different skin tones and BMI, with a contrast enhancement pipeline to improve vein localization for low-cost clinical catheterization.

  2. MeMeA
    Statistical Analysis of Multispectral NIR Images for Vein Detection
    Janak Dave, Sneha Chand, Gs Rahul, and 3 more authors
    In 2023 IEEE International Symposium on Medical Measurements and Applications (MeMeA), 2023
    Abs TL;DR HTML

    Vein localization is crucial for successful cannulation, but it can be challenging due to factors such as skin colour and Body Mass Index (BMI). While near-infrared (NIR) imaging is established for improving vein visibility, the optimal wavelength for patients with varying skin colours and BMIs has not been thoroughly studied. Thus, it is important to explore optimal wavelength selection to address these influencing factors. In this study, we developed and tested a method for enhancing vein contrast using Multispectral NIR imaging and evaluated the impact of skin colour and BMI on vein visibility in a sample of 61 subjects. Our study found that the optimal wavelength for vein visibility in the NIR region was 864 nm, and the proposed method effectively enhanced vein contrast. We also found that skin colour and BMI influenced vein visibility, with fair skin and lower BMI associated with better vein visibility. Our study demonstrates the potential of NIR imaging for enhancing vein visibility, and also emphasizes in considering skin colour and BMI as significant factors. However, there were some limitations of our study, as the majority of the age group consisted of the young population and it was not inclusive of babies and elderly people for whom venous access is difficult.

    Identifies 864 nm as the optimal NIR wavelength for vein detection through a 61-subject study, demonstrating that skin colour and BMI are significant factors affecting vein visibility.
